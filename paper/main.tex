\title{A Bayesian quadratic-hazard model for biomarker-driven mortality
and morbidity risk: optimal health levels with uncertainty}

\author[byu]{R. Richardson}
\author[bio]{[Co-author]}
\address[byu]{Department of Statistics, Brigham Young University, Provo, UT, USA}
\address[bio]{[Department], Brigham Young University, Provo, UT, USA}

\begin{abstract}
Insurers increasingly underwrite and price on repeatedly measured biological
markers---body mass index, blood pressure, glucose, lipids---yet standard
actuarial hazard models impose monotone or multiplicative marker effects that
cannot capture the U- and J-shaped mortality and morbidity risks pervasive in
epidemiology. We develop a Bayesian quadratic-hazard stochastic process model
that jointly describes (i) the irregular longitudinal dynamics of a biomarker
through a mean-reverting (Ornstein--Uhlenbeck) diffusion and (ii) a quadratic
hazard whose minimum identifies an age-varying risk-minimizing level
$\fzero(t)$---a ``physiological optimum''---with curvature $Q(t)$ and
mean-reversion $a(t)$ interpretable as stress-resistance and adaptive capacity.
Estimation exploits the analytically marginalized likelihood, a Kalman-type
moment recursion that integrates the quadratic hazard against the Gaussian latent
state in closed form; we embed this recursion in a Bayesian framework to obtain
full posterior uncertainty. The treatment delivers (a) credible bands on the
optimal trajectory $\fzero(t)$ and the latent risk components, and (b)
posterior-predictive, dynamically updated survival and hazard forecasts with
coherent uncertainty---quantities of direct relevance to biomarker-based
underwriting, wellness-program design, and the uncertainty margins required under
modern solvency and reporting regimes. We show that priors are essential for
identifiability rather than cosmetic: on monotone-risk markers the
maximum-likelihood estimator collapses the quadratic term, while
weakly-informative priors recover a stable, interpretable fit. The contribution is
not the quadratic-hazard form, which is established, but its Bayesian actuarial
treatment---coherent posterior uncertainty for the optimal biomarker level and for
dynamic risk forecasts, with the regularization that renders the optimum
identifiable. The methods are implemented in \texttt{R}, validated by simulation,
and illustrated on publicly available longitudinal survival data.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Life and health insurers have moved decisively toward pricing and underwriting on
biological information. Accelerated underwriting programs draw on laboratory
panels, electronic health records, and increasingly on wearable-device streams,
fuelled by rapid adoption of predictive analytics across the actuarial control
cycle \citep{Richman2021}; health, long-term-care, and critical-illness products
are exposed to the trajectories of risk factors such as body mass index (BMI),
blood pressure, glycaemia, and lipids, and to their links with morbidity onset
\citep{HabermanPitacco1999}. A common feature of these markers is that risk is
\emph{not} monotone in the marker value: both low and high BMI carry excess
mortality, blood pressure exhibits J-shaped associations, and similar U- or
J-shaped relationships are documented across a range of physiological indices.

This feature sits awkwardly with the actuary's standard toolkit. Proportional
hazards and generalized linear models encode a marker through a linear predictor
and therefore impose a monotone, multiplicative effect on the force of mortality;
aggregate mortality models such as Lee--Carter \citep{LeeCarter1992} operate at the
population level and do not use individual marker dynamics at all. Capturing a U-shape within a
proportional-hazards model requires \emph{ad hoc} basis expansions whose
coefficients carry no direct interpretation, and none of these approaches makes
use of the information in the \emph{dynamics} of a repeatedly measured marker:
its trend, its volatility, and the speed with which it returns to equilibrium
after a perturbation. Yet these dynamics are exactly what longitudinal insurance
data---irregularly spaced, frequently missing---contain. The actuarial literature
has long accommodated population heterogeneity through frailty models, in which an
\emph{unobserved} multiplier scales the force of mortality
\citep{VaupelMantonStallard1979,SuSherris2012,Pitacco2019}; the approach developed
here is complementary, modelling the dynamics of an \emph{observed} marker and the
shape of its relationship with risk.

The stochastic process model (SPM), or quadratic-hazard model, developed in
biodemography \citep{WoodburyManton1977,Yashin2007,Yashin2012PLR} addresses these
points directly. It couples a mean-reverting diffusion for the latent biomarker
with a hazard that is \emph{quadratic} in the marker, so that risk is minimized at
an age-varying ``optimal'' level and rises with deviation in either direction. The
model's components have transparent risk interpretations: the location of the
hazard minimum is a physiological optimum, the curvature measures sensitivity of
risk to deviation, and the mean-reversion rate measures the organism's capacity to
recover. The SPM has appeared in the actuarial literature
\citep{Yashin2012NAAJ,Yashin2016NAAJ}, but exclusively in a frequentist form that
yields point estimates and, at most, asymptotic standard errors.

For the uses that matter most to insurers, point estimates are not enough.
Biomarker-based underwriting and reserving feed quantities---risk margins under
Solvency~II, the risk adjustment under IFRS~17, economic capital---that are
fundamentally about \emph{uncertainty}, and the relevant uncertainty includes
estimation and model uncertainty in components, such as the optimal level
$\fzero(t)$, that are identified only indirectly from the data. A Bayesian
treatment is the natural vehicle: it propagates this uncertainty coherently into
every derived quantity, it regularizes weakly-identified components through
priors, and---as we show---it is in fact \emph{necessary} for identifiability when
the marker's risk is close to monotone, a regime in which the maximum-likelihood
estimator degenerates.

The novelty is therefore not the quadratic-hazard form, which is established in
biodemography and has appeared in the actuarial literature in frequentist form;
it is \emph{Bayesian actuarial inference} for the stochastic process model:
coherent posterior uncertainty for the optimal biomarker level $\fzero(t)$ and for
dynamic risk forecasts, and the regularization that renders the optimum
identifiable in the near-monotone regimes common in practice.

\paragraph{Contributions.} We make four contributions.
\begin{enumerate}
\item We give a Bayesian formulation of the quadratic-hazard SPM, built on the
analytically marginalized likelihood (Proposition~\ref{prop:recursion}), so that
no latent path need be sampled and inference is over a low-dimensional, fully
interpretable parameter vector.
\item We obtain \emph{credible bands} on the risk-minimizing trajectory
$\fzero(t)$ and the latent risk components $Q(t)$ and $a(t)$, turning the model's
indirectly-identified quantities into objects with calibrated uncertainty.
\item We provide posterior-predictive, dynamically updated survival and hazard
forecasts conditional on a partial biomarker history, and we translate these into
actuarial summaries (survival by deviation from optimal; landmarked individual
risk).
\item We document an identifiability phenomenon of practical importance: on
monotone-risk markers the maximum-likelihood estimator collapses the quadratic
term, whereas weakly-informative priors recover a stable and interpretable fit.
\end{enumerate}
The methods are implemented in \texttt{R} using automatic differentiation
(\texttt{RTMB}) and Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (\texttt{tmbstan})
\citep{TMB2016,RTMB,tmbstan2018}, validated by simulation, and illustrated on two
public cohorts spanning U-shaped (blood pressure) and monotone (bilirubin)
biomarker risk. A companion paper applies the methodology to a large cardiovascular
cohort under restricted access.

\section{The model}\label{sec:model}

Let $Y(t)$ denote a (transformed, standardized) biomarker at age $t$ for an
individual, and let $T$ be the age at the event of interest (death, or onset of a
morbidity). The latent marker follows a time-varying Ornstein--Uhlenbeck diffusion
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sde}
  \dd Y(t) = a(t)\,\bigl[Y(t)-\fone(t)\bigr]\,\dd t + b(t)\,\dd W(t),
  \qquad Y(t_0)\sim \mathcal{N}\!\bigl(\fone(t_0),\sigma_0^2\bigr),
\end{equation}
with $W$ a standard Wiener process and $a(t)<0$ so that the process mean-reverts to
the drift $\fone(t)$. The force of mortality is quadratic in the marker,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hazard}
  \mu(t,y) = \mu_0(t) + Q(t)\,\bigl[y-\fzero(t)\bigr]^2,
  \qquad Q(t)>0 .
\end{equation}

Each component carries an actuarial interpretation.
\begin{itemize}
\item $\fzero(t)$ is the \emph{risk-minimizing level}: at any age, the hazard is
smallest when the marker equals $\fzero(t)$. It is the ``optimal'' value an
underwriter or wellness program would target, and it need not coincide with the
population mean.
\item $Q(t)$ is the \emph{curvature}, or sensitivity of risk to deviation from the
optimum: a larger $Q$ means a narrower tolerance band and a steeper penalty for
being off-target. In biodemographic terms it measures stress-resistance or
robustness.
\item $a(t)$ is the \emph{mean-reversion rate}: how quickly the marker returns to
its drift after a shock, interpretable as recovery capacity or resilience.
\item $\fone(t)$ is the \emph{drift} or equilibrium trajectory of the marker
itself, and $b(t)$ its volatility. The gap $\fone(t)-\fzero(t)$ measures how far
the typical trajectory sits from the optimum, and contributes directly to risk
through \eqref{eq:hazard}.
\item $\mu_0(t)$ is a baseline hazard capturing risk unrelated to the modelled
marker.
\end{itemize}

For the illustrations we adopt parsimonious, age-linear forms anchored at a
reference age $t_{\min}$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:components}
\begin{aligned}
  a(t) &= a_Y + b_Y\,(t-t_{\min}), & \fone(t) &= a_{f_1}+b_{f_1}(t-t_{\min}),\\
  \fzero(t) &= a_{f_0}+b_{f_0}(t-t_{\min}), & Q(t) &= a_Q + b_Q\,(t-t_{\min}),\\
  \mu_0(t) &= a_{\mu_0}\exp\!\{b_{\mu_0}(t-t_{\min})\}, & b(t)&\equiv\sigma_1,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
with $a_Y<0$, $a_Q>0$, and a Gompertz-type baseline. Richer (spline or
covariate-dependent) forms slot into the same machinery. Observations of the
marker are made at irregular ages $t_1<\dots<t_n$ subject to measurement error,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:obs}
  y_j = Y(t_j) + \varepsilon_j, \qquad \varepsilon_j\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\tau^2),
\end{equation}
which nests the classical SPM (exact observations) as $\tau\to0$. Measurement
error is realistic for insurance markers and, as a by-product, stabilizes the
filtering recursion below.

%====================================================================
\section{The marginalized likelihood}\label{sec:lik}

The model in \eqref{eq:sde}--\eqref{eq:obs} is a joint model of a longitudinal
process and a time-to-event outcome, and its likelihood appears to require
integration over the unobserved path $\{Y(t)\}$. The quadratic-Gaussian structure,
however, makes this integral closed-form: conditional on survival and on the
observations to date, the latent state stays Gaussian, and its mean and variance
satisfy ordinary differential equations that already incorporate the
survival-selection effect. This is the key to tractable Bayesian computation.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:recursion}
Consider \eqref{eq:sde}--\eqref{eq:obs}. Between observation times, let $m(t)$ and
$\gamma(t)$ denote the mean and variance of $Y(t)$ given survival to $t$ and the
observations up to the most recent observation time. Then $Y(t)$ is conditionally
Gaussian, $(m,\gamma)$ solve
\begin{align}
  m'(t)      &= a(t)\,[\,m(t)-\fone(t)\,] - 2\,Q(t)\,\gamma(t)\,[\,m(t)-\fzero(t)\,],
               \label{eq:m}\\
  \gamma'(t) &= 2\,a(t)\,\gamma(t) + b(t)^2 - 2\,Q(t)\,\gamma(t)^2,
               \label{eq:g}
\end{align}
and the hazard of an individual known to be alive at $t$ with this conditional
law---the \emph{effective hazard}---is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mubar}
  \bar\mu(t) = \mu_0(t) + Q(t)\,\bigl\{[\,m(t)-\fzero(t)\,]^2 + \gamma(t)\bigr\}.
\end{equation}
At an observation $y_j$, writing $m^-,\gamma^-$ for the pre-observation moments,
the predictive density is $y_j\sim\mathcal{N}(m^-,\gamma^-+\tau^2)$ and the moments
update by the Kalman gain $K=\gamma^-/(\gamma^-+\tau^2)$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:update}
  m^+ = m^- + K\,(y_j-m^-), \qquad \gamma^+ = (1-K)\,\gamma^-.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

A derivation via the killed Fokker--Planck equation and Gaussian closure is given
in \ref{app:deriv}; see also \citet{WoodburyManton1977,Yashin2007}. Two points
deserve emphasis for the actuary. First, \eqref{eq:g} shows the conditional
variance is \emph{reduced} by the term $-2Q\gamma^2$: selection removes the more
deviated (higher-risk) lives, so survivors are less dispersed than the marginal
diffusion would suggest---an effect that a na\"ive two-stage analysis ignores.
Second, \eqref{eq:mubar} adds $Q(t)\gamma(t)$ to the hazard evaluated at the
filtered mean: uncertainty about the latent marker is itself a source of risk.

Collecting terms, the contribution of one individual observed at $t_1<\dots<t_n$
and then followed to an event or censoring age $T$ (event indicator $\delta$) is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:loglik}
  \log L = \sum_{j=1}^{n}\log\phi\!\bigl(y_j; m_j^-,\gamma_j^-+\tau^2\bigr)
           \;-\;\int_{t_1}^{T}\bar\mu(s)\,\dd s
           \;+\;\delta\,\log\bar\mu(T),
\end{equation}
with $\phi$ the normal density. Missing or irregular measurements require no
special treatment: the recursion simply integrates \eqref{eq:m}--\eqref{eq:g}
across whatever gap occurs and updates \eqref{eq:update} when a measurement is
present. We evaluate \eqref{eq:m}--\eqref{eq:g} and the cumulative hazard with a
fixed-step Runge--Kutta scheme between event/observation times.

\section{Bayesian inference}\label{sec:bayes}

Let $\theta$ collect the parameters of \eqref{eq:components} together with
$\sigma_0,\sigma_1,\tau$ (positivity enforced by log-parameterization, and $a_Y<0$
by sign constraint). The log-posterior is \eqref{eq:loglik} summed over individuals
plus $\log p(\theta)$. Because \eqref{eq:loglik} is a smooth, deterministic
function of $\theta$ once the recursion is unrolled, its gradient is available by
automatic differentiation and the posterior is amenable to Hamiltonian Monte
Carlo. We implement the log-likelihood once in \texttt{RTMB} and sample with the
no-U-turn sampler via \texttt{tmbstan} \citep{RTMB,tmbstan2018}; the same objective
yields the maximum-likelihood (or maximum-a-posteriori) estimate by quasi-Newton
optimization, so frequentist and Bayesian fits share a single code path.

\paragraph{Priors and identifiability.} We use weakly-informative, marginally
proper priors: normal priors on the log-scale and slope parameters centered at
neutral values with scales wide enough to be dominated by even modest data. These
priors are not merely a Bayesian formality. The quadratic term in
\eqref{eq:hazard} is identified by \emph{curvature} of risk in the marker; when the
empirical risk is close to monotone over the observed range---as it is for many
clinical markers---the curvature is weakly identified and the likelihood admits a
degenerate ridge along which $Q\to0$ and $\fzero$ drifts off to infinity, with the
hazard absorbed into the baseline $\mu_0$. Section~\ref{sec:pbc} exhibits exactly
this collapse for the maximum-likelihood estimator on real data, and
Section~\ref{sec:sim} shows it recurs in a small fraction of replicate fits even
on data simulated with a genuine U-shape. A weakly-informative prior on
$Q$ (and on the baseline slope) removes the degeneracy and returns a stable,
interpretable posterior in which the optimum is estimated---with appropriately wide
credible bands that honestly reflect how much the data constrain it. Model
comparison across nested component specifications uses WAIC / PSIS-LOO in place of
the AIC used in earlier frequentist SPM work.

\section{Actuarial quantities}\label{sec:actuarial}

A proportional-hazards regression on the marker, or a standard
shared-random-effects joint model, returns a single monotone association and a
relative risk: it does not locate an optimum, does not separate sensitivity to
deviation ($Q$) from recovery capacity ($a$), and propagates no uncertainty into
the optimum because none is estimated. The present model instead supplies, for a
biomarker-exposed portfolio and with coherent posterior uncertainty throughout:
(i) an age-specific optimal level $\fzero(t)$---an underwriting target and the
reference point for wellness-incentive design; (ii) the force of mortality as an
explicit function of deviation from that optimum, the basis for risk-class
definition and portfolio what-if analysis; (iii) dynamically updated
survival/hazard forecasts as new measurements arrive, the input to in-force
re-underwriting; and (iv) the uncertainty in all of these, the input to a risk
margin or risk adjustment. The posterior over $\theta$ induces these quantities
directly; we detail three.

\paragraph{(a) The optimal level and its uncertainty.} The trajectory
$\fzero(t)=a_{f_0}+b_{f_0}(t-t_{\min})$ is a linear functional of $\theta$, so its
posterior is immediate and we summarize it by a median curve and a 95\% credible
band (Figure~\ref{fig:f0sim}). This is the ``optimal health metric with
uncertainty'': the value at which mortality risk is minimized at each age, together
with a statement of how precisely it is known.

\paragraph{(b) Risk by deviation from optimal.} For an individual maintained at a
deviation $\delta$ (in marker standard deviations) from the optimum, the force of
mortality is $\mu_0(t)+Q(t)\delta^2$ and the corresponding survival function
$S_\delta(t)$ follows by integration; its posterior gives credible intervals for,
e.g., the probability of surviving a fixed horizon. These quantities price the
\emph{value of optimality} and the cost of deviation, inputs to wellness-incentive
design and to portfolio-level what-if analysis.

\paragraph{(c) Dynamic individual prediction.} Given a partial biomarker history to
a landmark age, the filtering recursion \eqref{eq:m}--\eqref{eq:update} yields the
conditional law of the latent state, and projecting \eqref{eq:mubar} forward gives
the predictive survival curve $S(t\mid\text{history})$ with a credible band
(Figure~\ref{fig:dyn}). This is the object required for dynamic underwriting and
for updating reserves as new measurements arrive.

\section{Simulation study}\label{sec:sim}

We simulate cohorts from \eqref{eq:sde}--\eqref{eq:obs} on a standardized marker
scale with $t_{\min}=65$, biennial measurements, administrative censoring at age
$95$, and parameters chosen to give a clear U-shaped risk and a moderate event rate
(43\% over follow-up; median 12 measurements per subject). Data are generated by a
fine-grid Euler--Maruyama scheme with a competing-risk event draw at each step, and
the marker is observed with measurement error---so the estimation target is
recovered from realistically irregular series.

\paragraph{Parameter recovery.} Table~\ref{tab:recovery} reports the
maximum-likelihood fit to a cohort of $2000$ subjects. All thirteen parameters are
recovered, with standardized errors within sampling tolerance; in particular the
optimal-level intercept and slope $(a_{f_0},b_{f_0})$ are recovered essentially
without bias. The baseline-hazard parameters are the least precisely estimated, as
expected: they trade off against the quadratic term given a finite number of
events.

\paragraph{Calibration.} We assess calibration by a frequentist coverage study:
$R=200$ cohorts of $N=500$ are simulated from the fixed truth, fit by maximum
likelihood, and the empirical coverage of nominal $95\%$ Wald intervals is recorded
for each parameter and for the $\fzero(t)$ band across the age grid
(Table~\ref{tab:coverage}). Five fits ($2.5\%$) failed by driving the quadratic
term to the boundary---the finite-sample manifestation of the identifiability
degeneracy of Section~\ref{sec:bayes}, and direct evidence that the phenomenon is
intrinsic to maximum likelihood rather than an artefact of any one dataset. Among
the $195$ valid fits, coverage of the optimal trajectory $\fzero(t)$---the headline
quantity---is at nominal across ages (pooled $0.951$). Coverage is likewise near
nominal for the well-identified parameters; the weakly-identified curvature slope
$b_Q$ ($0.86$) and baseline intercept $a_{\mu_0}$ ($0.90$) are mildly under-covered
by the asymptotic intervals. These are exactly the components for which the
posterior, regularized by weakly-informative priors, is better behaved and for
which credible intervals replace the Wald approximation in practice---reinforcing,
rather than undercutting, the case for the Bayesian treatment.

\paragraph{Comparison with standard models.} To make the contribution concrete we
contrast the SPM with conventional alternatives on the same simulated cohort,
entering each subject's last observed marker as a time-fixed covariate
(Figure~\ref{fig:compare}). A Cox model with a \emph{linear} marker returns a
significant but monotone effect ($\hat\beta=0.27$, $p<0.001$): it imposes
``more is worse'' and is blind to the lower arm of the U, the regime in which low
values are also hazardous. A Cox model with a penalized-spline marker recovers the
U-shape, but only as a static snapshot of the last measurement---it treats the
noisy marker as exact, ignores the longitudinal dynamics, and yields no
interpretable optimum with quantified uncertainty (one must read a minimum off the
fitted curve and bootstrap for an interval). The SPM estimates the risk-minimizing
level directly, $\hat\fzero=0.01$ $[-0.07,0.09]$ against a truth of $0$, with a
credible interval and using the entire trajectory and its measurement error. The
distinction from flexible (spline) joint models is therefore not the ability to
bend, but the interpretable, uncertainty-quantified \emph{optimum} and the use of
the marker's dynamics.

\includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{method_comparison.png}
\caption{Estimated log relative hazard as a function of the marker on the simulated
cohort. A linear Cox model (monotone) misses the lower arm; a penalized-spline Cox
model recovers the shape but offers no uncertainty-quantified optimum and ignores
the trajectory; the SPM returns the optimum $\fzero$ with a 95\% interval (truth at

\end{figure}

\section{Illustrations on public data}\label{sec:apps}

We illustrate the methodology on two public, fully reproducible cohorts that span
the two regimes of interest: a biomarker with a genuine U-shaped mortality risk
(systolic blood pressure), where the model locates an interior optimum, and a
monotone-risk marker (serum bilirubin), which serves as a stress test. Both ship
with the \texttt{R} \texttt{survival} package, so the analyses can be reproduced
without restricted data.

\subsection{A U-shaped marker: blood pressure and mortality}\label{sec:nafld}

We use the NAFLD cohort \citep[\texttt{survival::nafld};][]{survivalR}, taking
systolic blood pressure (SBP, standardized) as the marker, death as the event, and
age as the time scale. SBP is a textbook J-/U-shaped mortality marker---both low
and high pressure carry excess risk---and a routine underwriting variable. We
average repeated SBP readings within each one-year window to suppress
visit-to-visit measurement noise, retain subjects with at least three such yearly
values, and analyse a subsample of $2{,}991$ subjects with $519$ deaths and
$14{,}306$ measurements over ages $14$--$101$.

The model identifies the U-shape cleanly. The curvature is firmly positive and
bounded away from zero ($\hat a_Q=0.030$, SE $0.002$), in sharp contrast to the
monotone case below, and the estimated risk-minimizing level is an interior
optimum of $138$\,mmHg with a tight interval ($95\%$ CI $[137,139]$;
Figure~\ref{fig:nafld}). We report the confound-free specification with an
age-constant optimum and curvature: the age-\emph{trends} of the optimum and of
the curvature are only weakly identified from these data and are mutually
confounded (freeing both flips the sign of the optimum's age-slope), so an
age-constant summary is the honest one. The remaining components---mean reversion
$\hat a(t_{\min})=-0.45$ (SE $0.04$) and an age-increasing drift---are all
identified. This is the actuarially central case: a routinely underwritten marker
for which the model returns a calibrated optimal level and a quantified cost of
deviation.

\begin{figure}[t]\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{nafld_f0_sbp.png}
\caption{NAFLD cohort, systolic blood pressure. (A) Estimated hazard as a function
of SBP at age 60 (the U-shape), with the risk-minimizing optimum and its $95\%$
interval. (B) Observed SBP over age with the estimated optimal level and band.}
\label{fig:nafld}
\end{figure}

\subsection{A monotone marker: a stress test}\label{sec:pbc}

We contrast this with the Mayo Clinic primary biliary cholangitis (PBC) sequential
data \citep[\texttt{survival::pbcseq};][]{survivalR}, a standard public
benchmark for joint longitudinal--survival modelling \citep{Rizopoulos2016}. We take log serum bilirubin
(standardized) as the marker, death as the event, and age as the time scale; after
restricting to subjects with at least two measurements the analysis comprises $285$
subjects, $122$ deaths, and $1918$ measurements (median six per subject) over ages
$26$--$85$. Bilirubin is a severity marker whose risk is essentially monotone,
providing a deliberate stress test of the model's behaviour away from a clean
U-shape.

The maximum-likelihood estimator collapses, exactly as anticipated in
Section~\ref{sec:bayes}: the curvature is driven to the boundary
($\hat a_Q\to0$), the information matrix is singular, and no standard errors are
available. The maximum-a-posteriori fit under weakly-informative priors is stable
and interpretable. It estimates a non-trivial mean-reversion
($\hat a(t_{\min})=-0.027$, SE $0.011$) that weakens with age, a positive and
age-increasing curvature ($\hat a_Q=0.020$, $\hat b_Q=0.0009$, SE $0.0003$), and an
optimal bilirubin trajectory that sits at the low end of the observed range and
declines with age---from roughly $1.5$\,mg/dL at age $26$ toward $0.4$\,mg/dL at
$85$ (Figure~\ref{fig:pbc}). The credible band on $\fzero(t)$ is wide, correctly
reflecting that a monotone-risk marker pins down only that the optimum is low, not
its precise location. This is the desired behaviour: the model neither fabricates a
spurious interior optimum nor breaks down, and it reports its own uncertainty.

\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{pbc_f0_bilirubin.png}
\caption{PBC data: observed serum bilirubin (log scale) and the estimated
risk-minimizing trajectory $\fzero(t)$ with 95\% band, on the natural (mg/dL)
scale. The optimum tracks the low edge of the observed marker, as expected for a
monotone-risk marker.}
\label{fig:pbc}
\end{figure}

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:disc}

We have given a Bayesian quadratic-hazard stochastic process model for jointly
modelling an irregularly measured biomarker and a time-to-event outcome, with the
risk-minimizing level $\fzero(t)$, curvature $Q(t)$, and mean-reversion $a(t)$ as
interpretable, uncertainty-quantified targets. The analytically marginalized
likelihood makes Bayesian computation routine, and the resulting credible bands and
posterior-predictive forecasts speak directly to actuarial needs.

\paragraph{Relation to existing approaches.} The model differs from the dominant
shared-random-effects joint models, which posit a \emph{linear} marker effect on
the log-hazard, and from flexible Bayesian additive joint models that admit
nonlinear associations through penalized splines: the latter can represent a
U-shape but yield a free-form smooth with no single, named optimum and no
structural separation of optimum, curvature, and recovery. Our contribution is the
interpretable, structured parametrization---and credible bands directly on its
components---rather than nonlinearity \emph{per se}. Within the actuarial
literature it provides the Bayesian counterpart to the frequentist SPM
\citep{Yashin2012NAAJ,Yashin2016NAAJ}.

\paragraph{Actuarial implications.} The framework supplies, for a biomarker-exposed
portfolio, both a central estimate and a coherent uncertainty for each individual's
risk. Concretely, propagating the posterior over $\theta$ through the portfolio's
mortality/morbidity cash flows yields a predictive distribution of liabilities
whose dispersion is the non-hedgeable biometric risk; a cost-of-capital charge on
that dispersion is a Solvency~II risk margin, and a chosen quantile or
cost-of-capital quantity of it is an IFRS~17 risk adjustment, neither of which a
point estimate can supply \citep{EnglandVerrallWuthrich2019}. The same posterior
supports dynamic underwriting,
in which risk is re-evaluated as measurements arrive; and it formalizes
wellness-incentive design through the estimated optimum and the priced cost of
deviation in Table~\ref{tab:survdev}.

\paragraph{Limitations and extensions.} We modelled a single biomarker with
age-linear components; multivariate markers, covariate-dependent components (for
underwriting factors and genetic risk), nonsymmetric U-shapes, and calendar-time or
cohort effects for forecasting are natural and fit within the same marginalized
likelihood. Scaling the sampler to large portfolios benefits from vectorizing the
per-individual recursion. A companion paper scales the methodology to a large cardiovascular cohort with
multiple correlated markers and a genetic underwriting factor.

\section{Derivation of the moment recursion}\label{app:deriv}

Let $p(y,t)$ be the sub-density of the latent marker among individuals alive at
$t$ with a given observation history. Between observations $p$ evolves by the
Fokker--Planck equation of \eqref{eq:sde} with a killing term from the hazard
\eqref{eq:hazard},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fp}
  \partial_t p = -\partial_y\!\bigl[a(t)(y-\fone(t))\,p\bigr]
                 + \tfrac12 b(t)^2\,\partial_{yy}p
                 - \mu(t,y)\,p .
\end{equation}
Write $p(y,t)=c(t)\,\phi(y;m(t),\gamma(t))$ for a normalizing constant $c(t)$ (the
conditional survival). Substituting a Gaussian and matching the coefficients of
$1$, $(y-m)$, and $(y-m)^2$ on both sides of \eqref{eq:fp}---using that
$\mu(t,y)$ is quadratic in $y$, so the killing term preserves the Gaussian
form---gives the moment equations \eqref{eq:m}--\eqref{eq:g} together with
$c'(t)/c(t)=-\bar\mu(t)$ with $\bar\mu$ as in \eqref{eq:mubar}; integrating the last
relation yields the survival factor in \eqref{eq:loglik}. At an observation, Bayes'
rule for a Gaussian prior $\mathcal{N}(m^-,\gamma^-)$ and Gaussian likelihood
\eqref{eq:obs} gives the update \eqref{eq:update} and the predictive density
$\mathcal{N}(m^-,\gamma^-+\tau^2)$. See \citet{WoodburyManton1977} and
\citet{Yashin2007} for the original treatments. \qed
